The Words Are Stuck
Friday - February 8, 2019
Documenting feelings as I write.

Writing. It's difficult.

I'm currently trying to stammer out a quality essay for Legacies: An Asian American Anthology, for which I was accepted as both an illustrator and a writer. I'm grateful, and I want to do a good job, but the problem is: THE WORDS ARE STUCK.

Write. Stop. Think. Stop.

Write. Stop. Think. Stop.

Write. Stop. Think. Stop.

On and on and on...

I've been writing (...and stopping...and thinking) for a whole week now. The first draft is due in three days, on Monday (2/11/19).

How do I write in a way that is appealing, yet clear? I look at some of my past writing, and it doesn't seem that bad. Why am I so paralyzed now?

Especially the introduction. God, how do I start an introduction?

I don't want to be overly sentimental. I don't want to overexaggerate my feelings. Emotional intensity in writing is like photo-saturation: too heavy-handed, and the moment can become overdramatic. Yet, without feeling, the piece becomes entirely colorless, which is not accurate either.

The whole essay becomes an enormous balancing act, a challenge worthy of a tightrope walker.

And then there's the whole process of selecting details to include. So many factors! So much noise! We like to link events together as cause and effect, to simplify and delineate, but in reality, there might be a thousand subliminal factors that contribute to one decision. It is a difficult feat, picking out a clean narrative from this tangled, chaotic mess that is the real world.

Props to all you writers out there. You have my respect.

Update: 02/09/19, Saturday

Writing, it went okay today :) Perhaps because today is Saturday; perhaps because I had more time to work; perhaps because I went to a different library. Maybe I just had to ram my head against a wall for a whole week before something stuck. Whatever the case, it worked. The words are unclogged.

Update: 03/02/19, Saturday

I'm in the final stretch! The essay is 99% completed. Just a little polishing to do here and there and it'll be done. The writing process yesterday went a little bit like this:

1. This sucks, this sucks, this sucks, this sucks.

2. Words don't come easy. My brain feels like a fish thrashing out of water.

3. This sucks, but at least I have something down on the page. On second read, maybe it's okay.

4. Went to sleep.

5. Upon waking: Hey, this isn't as bad as I remembered. Was it really me who wrote this?

I think a night of sleep allowed me to distance myself from the memories of struggle the day before. The critical voice in my head calmed down because I read the essay feeling like someone else wrote it.

Anyway, that's me. It'd be interesting to see other people live-document their feelings as they write. Do words always flow? Was this beautiful sentence born out of intense struggle? It'd be cool if we could somehow highlight sentences in a book in order to see the writer's thought process behind them.
