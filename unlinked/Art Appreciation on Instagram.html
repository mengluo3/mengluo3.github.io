Art Appreciation in the Instagram Era

Pondering how mass availability shapes the way we experience art.

February 14, 2019

Masahisa Fukase • Self Portrait, 1992

Intense, right?

This is a self-portrait by Japanese photographer Masahisa Fukase. The first time I saw it, it felt like a slap to the face.

Now, I don't mean that I felt insulted. What I mean is that I experienced a physical, visceral reaction of shock. The sensation unsettled me. I do appreciate art, but rarely does it trigger a physical effect like this.

Which made me wonder: would this same image be as potent if I was scrolling past it on Instagram?

I stumbled upon the photograph at Freer Sackler Gallery of Art, in person, in a book, where it was spread across a page dedicated solely to this one photo. Compare that to my Instagram feed, where I follow almost 400 artists and photographers, each image just a point in my attention as I scroll to the next one.

The question reminded me of this one episode of the Sixth Tone podcast, titled "How Chinese Photographers Capture the Country." In the interview, the photo editors observed a trend in photojournalism: modern photographs are more obvious—more straightforward—because the audience no longer lingers on a photograph in attempt to decipher its meaning, the way they might have in an era before the internet.

The same concept applies to modern advice in writing as well. Concision is key when the readers are busy individuals limited on time. What can be compacted in one word must not be elaborated in five.

Which leads me down another path of thought: does the same 'limited time' concept apply to music as well? There is no platform where I can effortlessly "scroll" through songs, like images, and I don't listen to music to dig for information, so I don't "skim" through songs the way I might an article. It might have to do with the fact that music spans time, whereas visual mediums span space. Perhaps songs have shortened over time, but, at least for me, I listen to the songs in my Spotify playlists in their entirety before moving on to the next. If I were to experience Spotify the same way I experience Instagram, rarely absorbing a full piece, all the short clips of music would blur together into a streak of pure noise, none of the individual pieces leaving a particular imprint in my memory.

Don't get me wrong. I am not trying to condemn Instagram or any other social media. There are facets of these platforms that I appreciate deeply. I am just pondering: how has technology and mass availability shaped the way we appreciate art? While we are exposed to a wider breadth of artists, does quantity water down the depth of the experience?

I wonder.

Footnote: To be honest, I don't think this question is THAT important, but I do think it is interesting to mull over, and it is always worth pondering the ways tech shapes the contours of our lives.
